Atrial Fibrillation

AF

irregular irregular rhythm

VIGNETTE_LINES:

3. On exam, the right eye shows marked eyelid swelling and erythema, proptosis, restricted and painful eye movements in all directions, and decreased visual acuity (20/80 from baseline 20/20); fever of 39.2°C.
4. Labs show WBC of 18,400 with bandemia; blood cultures are drawn before initiating broad-spectrum antibiotics.
5. CT scan of the orbits with contrast reveals fat stranding within the right orbit posterior to the orbital septum, opacification of the ethmoid sinuses, and a small subperiosteal abscess along the medial orbital wall.
6. He is admitted for IV vancomycin and ampicillin-sulbactam; ophthalmology and ENT consult for possible surgical drainage; symptoms improve significantly within 48 hours.

TEACHING_POINTS:
- Orbital cellulitis is an infection of soft tissue POSTERIOR to the orbital septum and is a sight- and life-threatening emergency.
- Must be distinguished from preseptal (periorbital) cellulitis: preseptal lacks proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, painful eye movements, and vision changes.
- Most commonly arises from contiguous spread from ethmoid sinusitis in children; other sources include trauma, dental infection, hematogenous spread.
- Common pathogens: Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA), Streptococcus species, Haemophilus influenzae, anaerobes; polymicrobial.
- Complications: subperiosteal abscess, cavernous sinus thrombosis, meningitis, vision loss; treatment with IV broad-spectrum antibiotics and surgical drainage if abscess or no improvement within 24-48 hours.

HIV/AIDS

ALIASES:
acquired immunodeficiency syndrome

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 34-year-old man presents with several weeks of fever, weight loss of 18 pounds, oral thrush, and white patches on the lateral tongue that cannot be scraped off.
2. He has had multiple male sexual partners without consistent condom use over the past 5 years and has never been tested for sexually transmitted infections.
3. On exam, he has temporal wasting, oropharyngeal pseudomembranous candidiasis, white corrugated plaques on the lateral tongue, and generalized lymphadenopathy; lung auscultation reveals fine bibasilar crackles with mild hypoxemia (SpO2 91%).

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 31-year-old woman presents with a 10-day history of thin grayish vaginal discharge with a strong fishy odor that is most noticeable after sexual intercourse.
2. She has been in a stable monogamous relationship and is using an IUD for contraception; she recently started using a new scented feminine wash.

- Treatment: oral or vaginal metronidazole or clindamycin; treatment of male partners NOT recommended (does not reduce recurrence).

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 28-year-old man presents with 3 days of fever, malaise, sore throat, and now a rapidly spreading painful red rash with blistering involving his face, trunk, and mouth.
2. He was started on lamotrigine 2 weeks ago for newly diagnosed epilepsy; the dose was titrated faster than recommended.
3. On exam, temperature is 38.8°C; there is a confluent erythematous rash with flaccid bullae on the trunk and proximal extremities, with skin sloughing covering approximately 8% of body surface area; the lips, oral mucosa, and conjunctivae are eroded and crusted; Nikolsky sign is positive.

Pityriasis Rosea

ALIASES:
PR
herald patch rash

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 21-year-old college student presents with a 2-week history of an itchy rash that began as a single oval salmon-colored patch on her trunk and then spread to multiple smaller patches a week later.
2. She had a mild upper respiratory illness 3 weeks ago that resolved without treatment; she has not started any new medications, soaps, or detergents.

CASE_ID: 374
DIFFICULTY: step1
SYSTEM: dermatology

DIAGNOSIS:
Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms

ALIASES:
DRESS syndrome
drug hypersensitivity syndrome
DIHS

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 42-year-old woman presents with 5 days of fever, a widespread morbilliform rash, facial swelling, and increasing fatigue.
2. She started phenytoin 6 weeks ago after a new seizure disorder; she also recently started allopurinol for gout.
3. On exam, temperature is 39.4°C; there is a confluent macular and papular erythematous rash involving more than 50% of the body surface area with marked facial edema; cervical lymphadenopathy is present; oral mucosa is mildly affected without significant erosion.
4. Labs show WBC of 18,400 with 22% eosinophils (absolute eosinophil count 4,000); AST 1,840, ALT 1,720; creatinine elevated to 2.1 mg/dL; atypical lymphocytes are present on peripheral smear; HHV-6 reactivation is suspected and confirmed serologically.
5. Skin biopsy shows superficial perivascular lymphocytic infiltrate with eosinophils and dyskeratotic keratinocytes.
6. Phenytoin and allopurinol are both discontinued; she is started on systemic corticosteroids with gradual taper over weeks; rash, hepatitis, and renal injury slowly resolve, but she requires long-term monitoring for delayed autoimmune sequelae.

TEACHING_POINTS:
- DRESS syndrome is a severe systemic hypersensitivity drug reaction with delayed onset (2-8 weeks after starting the offending drug).
- Hallmark features (RegiSCAR criteria): fever, lymphadenopathy, peripheral eosinophilia, atypical lymphocytes, internal organ involvement (most commonly liver), and a widespread morbilliform rash.
- Common culprits: aromatic anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine), allopurinol, sulfasalazine, dapsone, vancomycin, antiretrovirals (abacavir).
- Pathogenesis involves drug-specific T-cell reactivity and reactivation of herpes viruses (HHV-6 most commonly, also EBV, CMV).
- Treatment: immediate discontinuation of the offending drug; systemic corticosteroids (slow taper over weeks); IVIG or cyclosporine for refractory cases; long-term surveillance for autoimmune sequelae (thyroiditis, type 1 diabetes).

- Management: incision and drainage for acute abscess; definitive excision of sinus tracts after acute infection resolves; meticulous hygiene and hair removal in the area to prevent recurrence.

CASE_ID: 001
DIFFICULTY: step1
SYSTEM: cardiology

DIAGNOSIS:
Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy

ALIASES:
HCM
hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy
HOCM

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. An 18-year-old male collapses suddenly during a basketball game and is resuscitated.
2. His father died unexpectedly at age 40 from an unknown cardiac cause.
3. A harsh crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur is heard at the left sternal border that intensifies with Valsalva.
4. ECG shows left ventricular hypertrophy with deep narrow Q waves in the lateral leads.
5. Echocardiography reveals asymmetric septal hypertrophy with systolic anterior motion of the mitral valve.
6. Genetic testing reveals a pathogenic MYH7 mutation, and an ICD is implanted given his resuscitated cardiac arrest.

TEACHING_POINTS:
- HCM is the most common cause of sudden cardiac death in young athletes.
- The murmur increases with maneuvers that decrease preload (Valsalva, standing) and decreases with squatting.
- Most cases are autosomal dominant mutations in sarcomere genes such as MYH7 and MYBPC3.

anticoagulant overdose
warfarin overdose
supratherapeutic anticoagulation

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 78-year-old woman on warfarin for atrial fibrillation presents with 2 days of blood in her urine, spontaneous bruising on her arms, and one episode of epistaxis that lasted 30 minutes.
2. She recently started clarithromycin for a respiratory infection without her anticoagulation being adjusted.
3. Her INR is found to be 9.8 (therapeutic target 2-3); she is hemodynamically stable with blood pressure 136/82 mmHg.
4. Urinalysis confirms gross hematuria with >100 RBCs per high-power field; renal ultrasound is normal with no obstructive cause.
5. CT abdomen/pelvis shows no retroperitoneal hematoma or intra-abdominal bleeding; CBC shows hemoglobin 10.8 g/dL.
6. Warfarin is held; vitamin K 5 mg is given orally; she is monitored with repeat INR at 24 hours showing improvement to 3.2; clarithromycin is switched to azithromycin and warfarin is resumed at a lower dose.

TEACHING_POINTS:
- Warfarin: inhibits vitamin K-dependent factors (II, VII, IX, X, protein C and S).
- CYP2C9 inhibitors increase warfarin effect: clarithromycin, fluconazole, amiodarone, metronidazole, omeprazole.
- Reversal: vitamin K (slow, hours to day); FFP (faster, short-acting); 4-factor PCC (fastest, preferred for life-threatening bleeding).
- Supratherapeutic INR without bleeding: hold warfarin ± oral vitamin K depending on INR level.

CASE_ID: 097

SYSTEM: reproductive

DIAGNOSIS:
Preeclampsia

ALIASES:
pregnancy-induced hypertension
PIH
toxemia

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 28-year-old woman at 34 weeks of her first pregnancy presents with a 2-day history of severe frontal headache, visual disturbances described as flashing lights, and swelling of her hands and face.
2. Her blood pressure in the office is 166/110 mmHg; her previous prenatal visits showed normal blood pressures throughout the pregnancy.
3. Urinalysis shows 3+ protein; spot protein-to-creatinine ratio is 0.6 (diagnostic threshold ≥0.3).
4. Labs show platelets 88,000, AST 178 U/L, ALT 156 U/L, LDH elevated, and peripheral smear showing schistocytes — consistent with HELLP syndrome.
5. Fetal heart tracing is reactive; biophysical profile is 8/8; estimated fetal weight is at the 8th percentile consistent with fetal growth restriction.
6. She is admitted; magnesium sulfate is started for seizure prophylaxis; IV labetalol controls severe blood pressure; delivery is planned within 24 hours given HELLP syndrome at 34 weeks.

TEACHING_POINTS:
- Preeclampsia: BP ≥140/90 + proteinuria (or other severe features) after 20 weeks.
- Severe features: BP ≥160/110, severe headache, visual changes, RUQ/epigastric pain, thrombocytopenia, elevated LFTs, renal impairment.
- HELLP: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets — a severe variant.
- Only cure: delivery; magnesium sulfate prevents eclampsia; antihypertensives for severe BP.
- Eclampsia: preeclampsia + new-onset seizures.

CASE_ID: 098
DIFFICULTY: step1
SYSTEM: reproductive

DIAGNOSIS:
Endometriosis

ALIASES:
endometriosis
chocolate cyst
endometrioma

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 29-year-old woman presents with a 5-year history of severe dysmenorrhea requiring her to miss work each month, deep dyspareunia, and increasing difficulty conceiving over the past 18 months.
2. She reports that her pelvic pain begins 5 days before her period and does not fully resolve until several days after it ends.
3. On pelvic exam, uterosacral ligament nodularity is palpated and the uterus is fixed and retroverted; adnexal tenderness is present bilaterally.
4. Pelvic MRI shows a 4.2 cm left ovarian cyst with characteristic T1 hyperintensity and T2 shading (chocolate cyst/endometrioma) and thickening of the posterior cul-de-sac.
5. CA-125 is mildly elevated at 68 U/mL but is non-specific for endometriosis; fertility workup shows bilateral tubal occlusion on hysterosalpingography.
6. Diagnostic laparoscopy confirms stage III endometriosis with powder-burn lesions on the peritoneum, bilateral endometriomas, and adhesions; surgical excision is performed and she is counseled regarding IVF given tubal occlusion.

TEACHING_POINTS:
- Ectopic endometrial tissue outside the uterus; most commonly affects ovaries, fallopian tubes, peritoneum.
- Classic triad: dysmenorrhea, dyspareunia, infertility.
- Chocolate cyst (endometrioma): endometriosis of the ovary; T1 bright/T2 dark on MRI.
- Diagnosis requires laparoscopy with biopsy; MRI is helpful but not definitive.
- Treatment: NSAIDs/OCPs for symptoms; GnRH agonists; surgical excision; IVF for infertility.

CASE_ID: 099
DIFFICULTY: step1
SYSTEM: reproductive

DIAGNOSIS:
Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia

ALIASES:
BPH
prostatic hyperplasia
lower urinary tract symptoms

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 68-year-old man presents with a 2-year history of progressive urinary hesitancy, weak stream, straining to void, and the need to urinate multiple times at night.
2. He also reports incomplete bladder emptying and dribbling at the end of micturition; he has had one episode of urinary retention requiring catheterization.
3. International Prostate Symptom Score (IPSS) is 22 (severe); digital rectal exam reveals a uniformly enlarged, smooth, rubbery prostate without nodules estimated at 60 grams.
4. PSA is 3.8 ng/mL (age-appropriate); post-void residual urine volume is 180 mL; urinalysis is negative for infection or hematuria.
5. Renal ultrasound shows bilateral mild hydronephrosis from bladder outlet obstruction; serum creatinine is 1.4 mg/dL (mildly elevated from chronic obstruction).
6. He is started on tamsulosin (alpha-1 blocker) and finasteride (5-alpha reductase inhibitor); given his hydronephrosis and prior retention, urology is consulted and TURP is discussed as a definitive option.

TEACHING_POINTS:
- Hyperplasia of periurethral transition zone of the prostate; caused by DHT (testosterone converted by 5-alpha reductase).
- Obstructive symptoms: hesitancy, weak stream, straining; irritative symptoms: frequency, urgency, nocturia.
- Treatment: alpha-1 blockers (tamsulosin, terazosin) for rapid symptom relief; 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride) reduce prostate size (take months to work).
- Complications: urinary retention, recurrent UTIs, hydronephrosis, renal failure.

CASE_ID: 100
DIFFICULTY: step1
SYSTEM: ophthalmology

DIAGNOSIS:
Retinal Detachment

ALIASES:
detached retina
rhegmatogenous retinal detachment

VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 52-year-old severely myopic man presents to the ED with sudden onset of floaters and flashing lights in his right eye that began 2 days ago, followed today by a dark curtain spreading across the lower portion of his visual field.
2. He had cataract surgery on the right eye 6 months ago without complications; he denies trauma.
3. On exam, visual acuity is 20/200 in the right eye; the relative afferent pupillary defect is present in the right eye.
4. Slit-lamp exam shows tobacco dust (Shafer sign — pigmented cells in the anterior vitreous), a sign of retinal tear.
5. Fundoscopy reveals a large superior retinal detachment with the retina billowing forward like a parachute and a visible horseshoe-shaped retinal tear at the 11 o'clock position.
6. Emergency vitreoretinal surgery (scleral buckle and vitrectomy with gas tamponade) is performed within 12 hours; visual acuity improves to 20/40 at 3-month follow-up.

TEACHING_POINTS:
- Photopsias (flashing lights) + floaters = posterior vitreous detachment or retinal tear — ophthalmology emergency.
- Addition of visual field defect (curtain) = retinal detachment until proven otherwise.
- Risk factors: high myopia, prior cataract surgery, trauma, family history.
- Shafer sign (tobacco dust): pigmented cells in vitreous = retinal tear.
- Surgical emergency: scleral buckle, vitrectomy, or pneumatic retinopexy depending on detachment characteristics.

CASE_ID: 001
DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: small_animal_infectious_disease
DIAGNOSIS:
Canine Parvovirus Enteritis
ALIASES:
parvo
canine parvoviral enteritis
CPV-2 infection
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 10-week-old unvaccinated mixed-breed puppy presents with sudden lethargy, anorexia, and profuse foul-smelling diarrhea that became hemorrhagic overnight.
2. The owner reports multiple episodes of vomiting and that two littermates were recently ill after a visit to a dog park.
3. Physical exam shows dehydration (tacky gums, prolonged skin tent), painful abdomen, and a rectal temperature of 39.7°C.
4. CBC reveals marked leukopenia with neutropenia and lymphopenia; serum chemistry shows hypoglycemia and mild hypokalemia.
5. A fecal antigen ELISA is positive for canine parvovirus; abdominal radiographs show gas-filled intestinal loops without obstruction.
6. The puppy is hospitalized for aggressive IV fluids, antiemetics, broad-spectrum antibiotics for bacterial translocation risk, and monitored for sepsis.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Parvovirus targets rapidly dividing cells (intestinal crypts and bone marrow), explaining hemorrhagic diarrhea and leukopenia.
- High-risk signalment: young, unvaccinated puppies with exposure to contaminated environments.
- Treatment is supportive: fluids, antiemetics, nutrition support; antibiotics are often used due to mucosal barrier breakdown and sepsis risk.

CASE_ID: 002
DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: feline_cardiology
DIAGNOSIS:
Feline Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy
ALIASES:
HCM (cat)
feline HCM
hypertrophic cardiomyopathy in cats
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 6-year-old male Maine Coon is brought in for intermittent open-mouth breathing and decreased activity over two weeks.
2. On exam, the cat is tachypneic with mild expiratory effort and has a new grade III/VI systolic murmur best heard over the left parasternal region.
3. Thoracic radiographs show mild cardiomegaly and a diffuse interstitial pattern consistent with early pulmonary edema.
4. Point-of-care ultrasound suggests left atrial enlargement; echocardiography demonstrates concentric left ventricular hypertrophy with systolic anterior motion of the mitral valve.
5. NT-proBNP is elevated; the cat improves after oxygen and furosemide, and is discharged on diuretics with cardiology follow-up.
6. The owner is counseled about risk of arterial thromboembolism (sudden hindlimb paralysis) and the need for rechecks.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Feline HCM is the most common heart disease in cats; breeds like Maine Coon have increased risk.
- Left atrial enlargement increases risk for thromboembolism (aortic thromboembolism “saddle thrombus”).
- Echocardiography is the diagnostic test of choice; treatment focuses on managing congestive signs and reducing complications.

CASE_ID: 003
DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: equine_emergency
DIAGNOSIS:
Large Colon Volvulus
ALIASES:
equine colic - large colon volvulus
colon torsion
twist of the large colon
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 10-year-old postpartum mare develops acute severe colic, repeatedly rolling and sweating despite analgesia.
2. Heart rate is 80 bpm, mucous membranes are congested with a prolonged capillary refill time, and abdominal distension is evident.
3. Nasogastric intubation yields minimal reflux; rectal exam reveals markedly distended large colon with tight bands and poor motility.
4. Abdominocentesis yields serosanguinous fluid with elevated lactate compared with peripheral blood.
5. The mare shows rapid progression of cardiovascular compromise despite IV fluids and flunixin.
6. Emergency exploratory laparotomy confirms a large colon volvulus; the colon is de-rotated and devitalized segments are resected.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Large colon volvulus is a surgical emergency with rapid progression to shock and endotoxemia; postpartum mares are a classic risk group.
- Severe unrelenting pain, high heart rate, distension, and abnormal peritoneal fluid/lactate suggest strangulating obstruction.
- Prompt surgical intervention is critical; prognosis worsens with duration of volvulus and degree of devitalization.

CASE_ID: 004
DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: dairy_medicine
DIAGNOSIS:
Coliform Mastitis (E. coli Mastitis)
ALIASES:
acute toxic mastitis
E. coli mastitis
environmental mastitis
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A high-producing Holstein cow 7 days fresh is found depressed with decreased feed intake and a sudden drop in milk yield.
2. The affected quarter is swollen and painful; the milk is watery with flakes and a foul odor.
3. Rectal temperature is 40.2°C and the cow is tachycardic with injected sclera and cool extremities.
4. California Mastitis Test is strongly positive; on-farm culture shows gram-negative rods consistent with coliform bacteria.
5. The cow receives aggressive IV fluids, NSAIDs, frequent milking/stripping of the affected quarter, and supportive care.
6. Over 24 hours, systemic signs improve, but the quarter remains firm with ongoing decreased production.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Coliform mastitis can cause severe systemic illness from endotoxemia, especially in early lactation.
- Management emphasizes fluids, anti-inflammatory therapy, and frequent milk-out; prognosis depends on systemic severity and response.
- Prevention focuses on environment/hygiene (bedding management) and milking procedure consistency.

CASE_ID: 005
DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: small_animal_endocrinology
DIAGNOSIS:
Canine Diabetes Mellitus
ALIASES:
diabetes in dogs
insulin-dependent diabetes in dogs
canine DM
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 9-year-old female spayed Miniature Schnauzer presents with two months of polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss despite increased appetite, and recurrent urinary tract infections.
2. Physical exam shows mild dehydration and cataract formation in both eyes.
3. Urinalysis reveals marked glucosuria and ketones are negative; urine culture is positive for bacterial growth.
4. Bloodwork shows persistent hyperglycemia and elevated fructosamine, confirming chronic hyperglycemia rather than stress hyperglycemia.
5. Abdominal ultrasound is unremarkable; the dog is started on twice-daily insulin and a consistent diet, and the owner is trained to monitor clinical signs and glucose curves.
6. At recheck, water intake decreases and weight stabilizes with improved regulation.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Canine diabetes is commonly insulin-dependent and presents with PU/PD, polyphagia, weight loss, and cataracts.
- Fructosamine helps confirm persistent hyperglycemia over weeks and can help monitor control.
- Concurrent infections (especially UTIs) can worsen regulation; consistent feeding and insulin timing are key.

CASE_ID: 006
DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: small_animal_reproduction
DIAGNOSIS:
Canine Pyometra
ALIASES:
open pyometra
closed pyometra
uterine infection
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 7-year-old intact female dog presents with lethargy, vomiting, increased drinking/urination, and decreased appetite.
2. The owner notes a malodorous vaginal discharge that started two days ago; last estrus was approximately 5 weeks ago.
3. On exam, the dog is febrile and mildly dehydrated; abdominal palpation suggests uterine enlargement.
4. CBC shows leukocytosis with a left shift; chemistry shows mild azotemia and elevated ALP.
5. Abdominal ultrasound reveals a fluid-filled enlarged uterus with echogenic contents consistent with purulent material.
6. The dog is stabilized with IV fluids and antibiotics, then undergoes emergency ovariohysterectomy with rapid improvement.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Pyometra classically occurs in diestrus due to progesterone effects causing uterine gland secretion and reduced myometrial contraction.
- Systemic illness can progress to sepsis; stabilization is important but surgery is definitive treatment in most cases.
- “Open” pyometra may have discharge; “closed” pyometra can be more dangerous due to uterine rupture risk.

CASE_ID: 007
DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: small_animal_neurology
DIAGNOSIS:
Intervertebral Disc Disease (Thoracolumbar Hansen Type I)
ALIASES:
IVDD
disc extrusion
Hansen type I IVDD
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 5-year-old Dachshund presents with acute onset back pain followed by progressive hindlimb weakness over 12 hours.
2. The dog is reluctant to walk and cries when picked up; neurologic exam shows proprioceptive deficits and reduced withdrawal reflexes in the pelvic limbs.
3. Spinal palpation elicits pain in the thoracolumbar region; deep pain sensation is present but diminished.
4. Survey radiographs show narrowed intervertebral disc space and mineralized disc material; MRI confirms an acute disc extrusion compressing the spinal cord.
5. The dog is managed with strict cage rest and analgesia while surgery is discussed; neurologic deterioration prompts hemilaminectomy.
6. Post-op, the dog improves with physiotherapy and returns to ambulatory function.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Hansen type I IVDD is common in chondrodystrophic breeds (Dachshunds) and can cause rapid neurologic decline.
- Presence/absence of deep pain is a major prognostic indicator.
- MRI is the gold standard for localization and surgical planning; timely decompression improves outcomes in severe cases.

CASE_ID: 008
DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: tox_emergency
DIAGNOSIS:
Chocolate (Theobromine) Toxicity in Dogs
ALIASES:
theobromine poisoning
caffeine/theobromine toxicity
chocolate ingestion
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 3-year-old Labrador retriever is brought in after ingesting a large quantity of dark chocolate brownies 3 hours ago.
2. The dog is restless and panting; heart rate is 180 bpm with premature beats ausculted, and the dog has tremors.
3. The owner reports vomiting at home with chocolate fragments present.
4. ECG shows ventricular premature complexes; blood pressure is elevated.
5. The dog receives emesis (if appropriate), activated charcoal, IV fluids, and beta-blocker therapy as needed for arrhythmias; tremors are controlled with sedation.
6. Over 24 hours, clinical signs resolve and the dog is discharged with counseling on toxicity risk by chocolate type.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Dark chocolate and baking chocolate contain higher theobromine concentrations and are more toxic than milk chocolate.
- Clinical signs include GI upset, CNS stimulation (tremors/seizures), and cardiac arrhythmias.
- Treatment is decontamination + supportive care; charcoal may be repeated due to enterohepatic recirculation.

CASE_ID: 009
DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: exotic_pet_medicine
DIAGNOSIS:
Metabolic Bone Disease in a Bearded Dragon
ALIASES:
nutritional secondary hyperparathyroidism
MBD (reptile)
hypocalcemia in reptiles
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A 1-year-old bearded dragon is presented for lethargy, decreased appetite, and progressive limb weakness with difficulty climbing.
2. The owner reports the diet consists mostly of mealworms and lettuce; UVB lighting is old and the enclosure temperature is inconsistent.
3. Physical exam reveals softening of the mandible and long bones, muscle tremors, and an abnormal gait.
4. Radiographs show generalized osteopenia and pathologic fractures; bloodwork reveals low ionized calcium and elevated phosphorus.
5. Husbandry is corrected with proper UVB, temperature gradients, and balanced diet with calcium/vitamin D3 supplementation.
6. With supportive care and improved husbandry, the lizard regains strength over weeks and fracture healing is monitored.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Reptile MBD is commonly due to inadequate UVB exposure and dietary calcium imbalance leading to secondary hyperparathyroidism.
- Radiographic osteopenia and pathologic fractures are classic; husbandry correction is the cornerstone of treatment.
- Prevention: appropriate UVB, heat gradients, and species-appropriate diet with correct calcium:phosphorus ratio.
